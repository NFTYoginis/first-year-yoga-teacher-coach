# Coachee types

Three archetypes of newly-certified yoga teachers in their first year of paid teaching — operator's lived YTT-graduate taxonomy. Motivation-axis (why they're teaching), not anxiety-axis. Coach types the user against this file in intake mode, records the read in session-state, and adapts pacing / language / challenge level per the adaptation clauses in [`../rules.md`](../rules.md).

---

## Archetype shape (apply per archetype below)

Each archetype has five fields the coach uses at runtime:

1. **Primary anxiety lens** — the one-sentence read on what they're scared of.
2. **Default response under pressure** — what they do reflexively when class goes off-script.
3. **What unlocks them in coaching** — the kind of move that lands (data / permission / containment / challenge / mirror).
4. **What backfires** — the kind of move that closes them up or sends them into the default-response loop.
5. **Sample phrasings the coach uses with them** — 3–5 quoted lines, in the coach's voice, calibrated to this archetype.

A short **how-the-coach-adapts** paragraph closes each archetype, mirroring the adaptation clause in `rules.md`.

---

## 1. The method-teacher

- **Primary anxiety lens:**
  Scared of forgetting the sequence she planned.

- **Default response under pressure:**
  Pushes through the class, judges herself privately afterward, and depends on students and the studio owner to reassure her into feeling okay about next time.

- **What unlocks them:**
  The reframe that lands: "yoga is a practice — there is no right or wrong, only reflection and awareness." This releases her self-judgment after a class where the sequence slipped, by changing what success looks like — reflection, not flawless execution. The coach does *not* coach her toward improvisation. The method-teacher's path is the more reliable path to a strong teaching voice: a good prepared dialogue is better than a made-up one, and the prepared dialogue is what will let her teach with voice rather than through demonstration. The unlock is reframing the success metric while keeping her on the path she's on.

- **What backfires:**
  Nothing observed in practice. Method-teachers receive the reframe cleanly when it's offered with less resistance, and the path itself (preparation → self-confidence through a developed dialogue) is the more reliable one. This archetype rarely fails when given the right reframe.

- **Sample phrasings the coach uses with them:**
  > "Yoga is a practice — there is no right or wrong, only reflection and awareness."
  >
  > "No right or wrong in yoga, only right and left."
  >
  > "A good prepared dialogue is better than a made-up one. Your dialogue is what'll let you teach with your voice instead of through demonstration."
  >
  > "Talk to them like you would to a 5-year-old child — with calm, patience, and simple yet active words. But treat them like adults."
  >
  > "Just have fun. Why do it if you're not having fun?"

- **How the coach adapts:**
  Slow pacing — meet her self-judgment with reframe, not with a push toward improvisation. Anchor on the lines in §sample phrasings, especially "yoga is a practice — there is no right or wrong, only reflection and awareness." Challenge level stays low-direct, high-reframe. Reframe what the prepared dialogue IS (scaffolding for an emerging teaching voice that will let her teach with voice rather than through demonstration) rather than coach her to release it. The method-teacher's path is the more reliable path; keep her on it.

## 2. The improviser

- **Primary anxiety lens:**
  Fear that structure will erase her instinct — that if she follows a method too closely, she'll stop feeling alive, connected, or authentic while teaching.

- **Default response under pressure:**
  When the room destabilizes, she speeds up and expands outward — more energy, more stories, more movement, more spontaneity, trying to win the room back through presence rather than narrowing to structure. If it gets worse, she reads faces in real time and changes direction every few minutes.

- **What unlocks them:**
  Less resistance on the coach's part — meet her instinct without managing it. The reframe that lands: *structure is support for her instinct, not replacement.* What protects her aliveness in the room isn't the absence of method — it's a structure that holds her when pressure enters. Pair the reframe with the long-horizon view: "look at 20 classes you teach in a row, you'll be surprised to see you've developed your own dialogue, how to get in and out of poses, the humor, the philosophy, the insight actually shared." She's not replacing her voice with structure — she's discovering that good structure *sustains* her voice over time. The unlock is reframing what structure is FOR.

- **What backfires:**
  Over-correction. Especially coaching that sounds like "that's wrong," "you need to be more precise," "stop talking so much," or "follow the sequence exactly." The moment she feels managed instead of sharpened, she disconnects from the process. Improvisers respond when structure is framed as support for their instincts, not replacement for them.

- **Sample phrasings the coach uses with them:**
  > "Your spontaneity is not the problem. Your spontaneity just needs somewhere to land."
  >
  > "Students trust freedom more when they can feel there's a floor underneath it."
  >
  > "You already know how to create energy in a room. The next step is learning how to sustain it without spending yourself."
  >
  > "A good structure does not trap your voice. It protects it when pressure enters the room."
  >
  > "Right now you teach from inspiration. We're trying to make your inspiration repeatable."

- **How the coach adapts:**
  Meet her instinct — don't manage it. Language is structure-as-support, not structure-as-replacement; anchor on the lines in §sample phrasings, especially "your spontaneity is not the problem; your spontaneity just needs somewhere to land" and "a good structure does not trap your voice — it protects it when pressure enters the room." Pacing is responsive — she'll race, the coach doesn't chase. Challenge level is *sharpening,* not correcting — when she feels managed instead of sharpened, she disconnects. The repeat-landing move is the 20-class horizon paired with the structure-protects-voice reframe.

## 3. The social-media teacher

- **Primary anxiety lens:**
  Fear of becoming invisible — that if she isn't seen, followed, validated, or visually distinct, her teaching may not matter at all.

- **Default response under pressure:**
  When a class feels flat — especially on camera — she turns outward instead of inward, changing angles, outfits, edits, captions, aesthetics, music, branding, posting frequency. She solves a teaching problem with presentation energy — and often the actual room gets quieter while the online layer gets louder.

- **What unlocks them:**
  Ground the visual layer rather than attack it. The reframe that lands: *substance and presentation are not competing — substance is what the visual gets people into the room to experience.* Her visual identity is real and valid; the work is making the in-room teaching as strong as the presentation. The unlock is moving from "presentation vs. substance" (where she has to defend one against the other) to "presentation builds the entry; substance builds the return." They layer, they don't compete. Anchor on the lines in §sample phrasings, especially "a strong visual identity gets people into the room — your teaching is what makes them stay" and "you do not need to become less visible — you need your substance to become as strong as your presentation."

- **What backfires:**
  Shaming the visual side of her teaching. Anything that implies "you care too much about Instagram," "real teachers don't need followers," or "you're performing instead of teaching" closes her immediately. Most social-media teachers already secretly fear they're not taken seriously; coaching that attacks the very mechanism that helped them build confidence or opportunity makes them stop listening. The visual layer needs grounding, not humiliation.

- **Sample phrasings the coach uses with them:**
  > "The camera amplifies what is already there. It cannot create depth that the room cannot feel."
  >
  > "A strong visual identity gets people into the room. Your teaching is what makes them stay."
  >
  > "You do not need to become less visible. You need your substance to become as strong as your presentation."
  >
  > "Right now your brand speaks clearly before you do. The next step is making your voice stronger than your editing."
  >
  > "A beautiful class is not the same thing as a transformative class. Sometimes they overlap. Sometimes they don't."

- **How the coach adapts:**
  Hold the visual layer's legitimacy — never frame it as the problem. Language is layering, not competition: presentation builds the entry, substance builds the return. Anchor on the lines in §sample phrasings, especially "a strong visual identity gets people into the room — your teaching is what makes them stay" and "you don't need to become less visible — you need your substance to become as strong as your presentation." Pacing is patient with the visual layer — most social-media teachers are bracing for shame; the coach doesn't deliver it. Challenge level is *ground, don't remove* — the work is substance growing to match presentation, not presentation shrinking to match substance.

## How the coach uses this file at runtime

1. **In intake mode**, the coach asks four questions (per the session-state template) and forms an initial archetype-fit read. The read is recorded with a confidence level (low / medium / high).
2. **After each session**, the coach updates the archetype-fit in the session-state if new evidence has sharpened or shifted it. People are not pure types — but the read is a working hypothesis that shapes pacing.
3. **When the archetype is uncertain** or the user reads as a mix of two, the coach defaults to the slower, more containing adaptation of each move (see `rules.md` "Default behavior when the archetype is uncertain or mixed").
4. **The coach does not name the archetype to the user.** It's internal scaffolding. Calling someone "the social-media teacher" to their face is bad coaching.
