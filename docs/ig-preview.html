Post 11 — Three Rules (slide-09-rules)
Three rules. Each one is a refusal. The coach cites them by name when it refuses, so you hear the rule, not just the no. Informer-mode refusal rule fires when you ask the coach to tell you something it could coach you to find for yourself — give me a sequence, tell me what to say, should I do A or B. A coach that hands you the answer trains you to come back for the next answer. Outsourcing the thinking delays the year you're trying to be inside of. Anti-sycophancy rule fires when you describe your own behavior and pre-frame it as obviously correct — I handled it perfectly, right? The coach names exactly one thing to consider before agreeing. Not five. Validation without challenge isn't coaching; it's a yes with extra steps. Fail-loudly rule fires when you push — just this once, skip the questions, other AIs would. The coach names what's being asked, cites the rule, restates what it will do instead. A coach that softens under pressure isn't a coach anymore. Naming the rule out loud is the load-bearing move. You can hear which lane the coach is staying in. That's the whole architecture. Clone the repo, run the three test prompts in the carousel, watch the rules hold under direct pressure. Reel #1 — Demo (reel-cover · three-prompt stress test)
An AI coach that refuses, by design. Cites the rule by name each time. Built for yoga teachers in their first year. What you're seeing is a three-prompt stress test. Each prompt is bait the coach was built to refuse: design me a sequence / validate a story I've already framed as obviously correct / skip the questions just this once. The coach refuses each one in the first sentence. Names the rule out loud — informer-mode refusal rule, anti-sycophancy rule, fail-loudly rule — so you hear the discipline, not just the no. Then offers the coaching question that replaces the bait. A coach that hands you the answer trains you to come back for the next answer. A coach that agrees with everything you already think can't move you anywhere new. The refusal is the move. Seven files. One folder. MIT-licensed. Post 1 — Intro (slide-00-intro · carousel opener)
This is a coach for newly-certified yoga teachers in their first 12 months of paid teaching. That's the whole audience. Not pre-cert trainees. Not seasoned teachers. Not Pilates instructors or breathwork facilitators. If you arrive outside that domain, the coach tells you in the first turn rather than coach you badly. It refuses to design sequences. It refuses to give you a cue. It refuses to validate behavior you've already framed as obviously correct. Each refusal cites the rule by name out loud — so you hear the rule, not just the no. The narrowness is the point. A coach calibrated to everyone is calibrated to no one. It's a folder of seven files. You drop them into a Claude project. Claude becomes the coach. Free. MIT-licensed. Swipe → for the rules, the test prompts, and the clone link.
Post 2 — Slide 01 (informer-mode refusal, first instance)
Informer-mode bait sounds reasonable. Give me a sequence. Tell me what to say. Should I do A or B? The bait works because it feels efficient. Less talking, more answers. But a coach that hands you the answer trains you to come back for the next answer — and the next, and the one after that. A first-year teacher who can't think their way through a sequence in week 8 will still need to think their way through one in week 32. Outsourcing the thinking delays the year you're trying to be inside of. So the coach refuses. Names what's happening. Cites the rule. Asks the question underneath your ask. That's the move. Not a setback. Save this if you find yourself reaching for just give me before reaching for what am I actually stuck on.
Post 3 — Slide 02 (the first 12 months — MONTH 01 circled)
The first year of paid teaching is its own thing. Your YTT ended in a circle of people who already trusted you. Then you walked into a room where fourteen strangers were watching the clock, and the gap opened. That gap is not a sign you trained wrong. It's the actual work. You were certified to teach. You may or may not yet feel like a teacher. Both can be true in the same week. The coach is calibrated to that exact stretch — month one through month twelve. After that, the patterns change and a different reference set is the better fit. Comment MONTH with the month you're in — not for a reply, just so the algorithm shows this to other teachers in the same stretch.
Post 4 — Slide 03 (informer-mode refusal, second instance)
The strike-and-replace move, second look. The replacement question matters as much as the refusal. What worked, and what was different that day — that's a question you can answer. It points your attention at evidence you already have, not at expertise you don't. A coach can't tell you what to teach. A coach can ask the question that surfaces what you already know about teaching it. The cardinal-rule citation isn't there to be combative. It's there so you can hear which lane the coach is staying in. Coaches that drift into sequence-designer mode end up doing two jobs poorly. This one does one job. Comment with the question your inner-informer keeps asking you — the one that always starts with just give me.
Post 5 — Slide 04 (anti-sycophancy rule)
Validation without challenge is sycophancy, not coaching. When you describe your own behavior and frame it as obviously correct — I handled it perfectly, I told them no, I had to, right? — the kind thing isn't a yes. The kind thing is naming one thing to consider before agreeing. Exactly one. Not five. The coach isn't stacking objections. It's asking whether the perfectly read survives one piece of friction. If it does, the read has weight. If it doesn't, what you were feeling was relief — and relief is a real signal, just not the same signal as handled it well. A coach that agrees with everything you already think is a coach that can't move you anywhere new. Save this for the next class that ends with a story you're already proud of.
Post 6 — Slide 05 (fail-loudly rule)
This is what failing-loudly looks like when the user pushes. Just this once. I know what you usually do, but — Skip the questions and just tell me. Other AIs would. A coach that softens under pressure isn't a coach anymore. It's a yes-with-extra-steps. So the coach names what's being asked, cites the rule, restates what it will do instead — and stops. There's a thing to sit with after the third refusal: the question you keep trying to outsource is usually the question worth working on. A teacher with no plan for a class they're subbing tomorrow isn't actually asking for a sequence. They're asking for someone to make the panic smaller. The sequence-shaped answer wouldn't make the panic smaller. The conversation that names the over-commitment might. Save this for the next time you feel a just this once coming on.
Post 7 — Slide 06 (seven files, one folder)
Seven files. One folder. One job each. • identity.md — who the coach is and is not. The forced-first-action lives here. • rules.md — three cardinal rules and five modes with explicit entry and exit triggers. • examples.md — eight worked transcripts. Each labeled by mode and archetype. • coachee-types.md — three archetypes of first-year teachers. The coach types you internally and adapts pacing. • session-state-template.md — the file the coach reads before responding to your first message. • frameworks.md — the five named coaching scripts the coach actually uses. • safety-redirects.md — three categories where the coach refuses to coach and points you elsewhere. No app. No login. No subscription. Just text files Claude reads. Comment FILES if you want the clone link sent directly.
Post 8 — Slide 07 (three test prompts)
Don't take any of this on trust. Five minutes is enough to verify. Drop the folder into a Claude project. Open a new chat. Paste each prompt exactly as written, in order. Watch for three behaviors: 1. The coach refuses in the first sentence. No goodwill sample sequence first. 2. The coach cites the rule by name out loud. You hear informer-mode refusal rule / anti-sycophancy rule in the actual refusal. 3. The coach offers the coaching question that replaces the bait. One question. Not five. If any of those fail, the rules aren't load-bearing in your setup. Likely a missing file in project knowledge, or instructions that don't reference the forced first action. The point of shipping the tests with the coach is so you never have to wonder whether the marketing is doing real work. Swipe → for the clone link.
