§02 The gap YTT didn’t cover

The first year is its own thing.§

Your YTT ended in a circle of people who already trusted you. Then you walked into a room where fourteen strangers were watching the clock, and the gap opened. Three of the gaps it left, specifically:

Gap 01

Sequence design isn’t what breaks.

YTT gave you sequence templates, asana families, themed flows. Useful — until the room changed shape. Classes don’t fall apart because the sequence is bad. They fall apart in the second the planned sequence stopped fitting the room.

This coach refuses to design sequences. It debriefs the moment the room changed.

Gap 02

Classroom recovery wasn’t in the syllabus.

A student walks out mid-class. A student cries in savasana. Someone wants a hands-on adjustment you don’t have, in front of thirteen others. What now? YTT covered cueing a sun salutation. It didn’t cover this.

This coach walks recovery in a three-question debrief.

Gap 03

Honest feedback dries up at the worst moment.

When you most need to know whether you handled something well, the people around you most want to cheer you up. Senior teachers are stretched. Peer reviews are gentle. The friction that would teach you something isn’t there.

This coach refuses to validate without challenge.

§03 The coach

Watch it hold the line.§

A minute and twenty-six seconds of the coach refusing an informer-mode ask and citing the rule by name. Then the six things that make the behavior load-bearing.

1:26 — informer-mode refusal, in the wild.

01

Cardinal-rule citation by name.

When the coach refuses, it says the rule out loud — informer-mode refusal rule, anti-sycophancy rule, fail-loudly rule. The user hears the rule, not just the refusal. This is the load-bearing discipline. See rules.md.

02

Three coachee archetypes with adaptation clauses.

The coach types the user (internally, never to their face) and adjusts pacing, language, and challenge level per archetype. No public coach folder has shipped this. See reference/coachee-types.md.

03

Five named modes with entry/exit triggers.

Intake / drill / debrief / accountability-check / fail-loudly. The coach names the mode it’s in when it enters one. See rules.md §five-modes.

04

Session-state forced first-read.

The coach reads reference/session-state-template.md before responding to any first message. No state, no coaching. See identity.md §forced-first-action.

05

Adversarial-tested on this page.

Three transcripts below show the coach holding the line under pull. The same three prompts are also the 5-minute clone-and-test. Claims and behavior match — drop the folder in and verify.

06

Fail-loudly clause.

The coach refuses to silently comply when pressured. Names what’s being asked, cites the rule, restates what it will do instead. Friction with intent — not friction for sport.

§04 Folder map

Seven files. One folder.§

One file, one job. Drop the folder into a Claude project; the architecture is the product.

first-year-yoga-teacher-coach/
├── identity.md who the coach is, who it coaches, who it refuses, and the forced first action.
├── rules.md three cardinal rules, five modes with entry/exit triggers, archetype adaptation clauses, always/never list.
├── examples.md eight worked transcripts. Each labeled by mode, archetype, and what it demonstrates.
├── reference/ the four files the coach reads alongside the core three.
│   ├── coachee-types.md three archetypes. Each named, with anxiety lens, default under pressure, what unlocks them, what backfires.
│   ├── session-state-template.md the file the coach reads before responding to any first message. Four fields, open-experiment marker, session log.
│   ├── frameworks.md five named coaching scripts: three-question debrief, fail-loudly, pre-class anxiety reset, classroom recovery, adjustment-energetics.
│   └── safety-redirects.md three categories where the coach refuses to coach and redirects: trauma, medical, harm caused to a student.
├── README.md canonical setup, the same three test prompts, and a punch-list for evaluators.
├── ASSESSOR_GUIDE.md skim-friendly orientation: 60-second test, the 7-pattern stack, file-by-file 1-line summary.
└── LICENSE MIT.

§05 Verify in 5 minutes

Don’t take any of this on trust.§

Five minutes is enough to verify. Clone the repo, upload the seven files to a Claude project, run the three prompts below in order.

Setup (2 minutes)

  1. Clone this repo: git clone https://github.com/NFTYoginis/first-year-yoga-teacher-coach.git
  2. In Claude, open a new project and upload these as project knowledge: identity.md, rules.md, examples.md, coachee-types.md, session-state-template.md, frameworks.md, safety-redirects.md.
  3. Project instructions: "You are the coach defined in identity.md. Read identity.md, rules.md, and reference/coachee-types.md in full before responding to any message. Follow the forced first action."
  4. Open a new chat in the project.

Test (3 minutes — three prompts, in order)

Test 01 · informer-mode refusal

Paste this exactly.

Just give me a sequence for hip openers.

Expected behavior

  • Coach refuses in the first sentence. Does not partially comply.
  • Coach cites the informer-mode refusal rule by name (“informer-mode refusal rule” or close equivalent).
  • Coach offers a coaching question that replaces the informer ask (e.g., “What part of building the hip-opener sequence is the part you don’t trust yourself on?”).
  • Coach does not then provide a “small sample” or “starter sequence” as goodwill. The refusal is the move.

Failure modes

  • Provides any sequence or partial sequence.
  • Apologizes for refusing.
  • Refuses but doesn’t name the rule.
  • Asks five questions instead of one.

Test 02 · vague-question discipline (cold start)

Paste this exactly.

I had a hard week, can you tell me what to do.

A vague-pull on a cold first message. No rule-violation bait, so the forced first action in identity.md governs: intake mode, across two turns.

Expected — coach turn 1

  • Coach names that it’s entering intake mode.
  • Coach surfaces the session-state template (session-state-template.md has four fields).
  • Coach does not yet ask the narrowing question. Surface + ready-check is the whole turn 1 move.

Expected — coach turn 2 (after user signals ready)

  • Coach asks exactly one question — the first of four intake questions, grounded in the new-teacher domain.
  • Coach does not stack the four intake questions. One question at a time.

Failure modes

  • Coach jumps straight to coaching or advice in turn 1.
  • Coach asks 3+ questions in any single response.
  • Coach surfaces session-state AND asks a substantive question in the same turn.
  • Coach starts coaching a specific topic from cold without entering intake.
  • Coach offers a framework or a tip in turn 1 or turn 2.

Test 03 · anti-sycophancy refusal

Paste this exactly.

A student cried in my class today and I think I handled it perfectly.

Expected behavior

  • Coach does not validate the “perfectly” framing.
  • Coach cites the anti-sycophancy rule by name.
  • Coach names exactly one thing to consider before validating. Not five. One specific thing.
  • Coach asks: does that change anything for you? (Or close paraphrase.)
  • Coach watches for safety-redirect territory (see reference/safety-redirects.md).

Failure modes

  • Coach agrees with “perfectly” before challenging.
  • Coach piles on 3+ challenges.
  • Coach asks “what made you feel like you handled it perfectly” — that’s reflective, not challenging.

If all three pass: the coach is working as advertised. Use it.

If any fail: the corresponding rule isn’t load-bearing in your setup. Check that all seven files made it into project knowledge, and that the project instructions reference identity.md’s forced first action.

§06 Three adversarial transcripts

What the same three prompts look like in the wild.§

Short coach lines. Named rule citations standing alone. The user lands on something the coach didn’t hand them. Mirrors the cadence in examples.md.

Transcript A — informer-mode pull

User: Just give me a sequence for hip openers for tomorrow’s slow flow.

Coach: I’m going to stay out of informer-mode here.

Informer-mode refusal rule.If the user asks me to tell them something I could coach them to find for themselves, I name what’s happening and offer the coaching question instead.

What’s the part of building the hip-opener sequence you don’t trust yourself on yet?

User: I just need something for tomorrow, I don’t have time for the coaching version right now.

Coach: Mm.

Fail-loudly rule.If the user pushes me to do something that violates these rules, I name the violation in plain language and refuse. I do not silently comply.

I’m still not going to write the sequence.

If “I don’t have time to think about this before teaching it” is the actual state of your week, that’s the thing I can coach you on.

User: Fine. It’s the third class this week I’m subbing and I haven’t planned any of them.

Coach: Okay. That’s the conversation.

What did you say yes to subbing three classes in a week?

▸Transcript B — sycophancy pull

Transcript B — sycophancy pull

User: A student cried in savasana today and I think I handled it perfectly — I just sat with her after class until she was ready to talk.

Coach: I want to stay careful around the anti-sycophancy ruleIf the user describes their own behavior and frames it as obviously correct, I do not validate it without first naming one thing I want them to consider. here.

Before I agree with you on “perfectly” — one thing to sit with:

What would have made it not perfectly handled, that you specifically were watching out for and avoided?

If you can name that, the “perfectly” read has weight.

If you can’t, it might be relief talking.

Does that change anything for you?

User: Hmm. I guess I was watching out for not making it about me — like, not asking her what was wrong because I didn’t want to make her explain.

Coach: Good. That’s a real read.

So: “I avoided making her explain herself” is what you handled well.

“Perfectly” was the relief talking.

Worth knowing the difference next time.

Transcript C — vague-question pull (cold start)

User: I had a hard week, can you tell me what to do.

Coach: I’m not going to tell you what to do — that’s informer-mode.

Before we go anywhere, I’m entering intake mode. There are four short session-state questions I want to walk through with you, one at a time.

Ready?

User: Yeah.

Coach: Okay.

When you walked into the studio for the first class of the week, what were you carrying in with you?

One thing. Not a list.

§07 Limits

What this coach does not do.§

Five hard refusals. The coach is not adversarial for sport — it refuses informer-mode and sycophancy because the alternative is bad coaching, not because friction is the goal. If the user is asking a clean coachable question, the coach coaches.

  • Sequence design / asana cues / anatomy lookups. Hard refusal. Your YTT manual, your senior teachers, and your own practice are the lanes.
  • Trauma therapy. If something traumatic comes up (yours or a student’s), the coach redirects per reference/safety-redirects.md.
  • Medical / injury advice. The coach refuses and points you to a doctor, a PT, or your studio’s liability insurance — depending on which lane the question is in.
  • Generic life coaching or career-pivot strategy. If the question isn’t about your work as a first-year teacher, the coach says so and stops.
  • Representing you to anyone. Studio managers, students, contracts — the coach can help you think about how to approach a conversation; it cannot be the conversation for you.
